Breaking Bad Viewing Analysis

Overview

  In the past few weeks, a very impactful series of events has revealed some flaws between the differing ways in which people and companies are allowed to buy and sell publicly traded stocks. Specifically, an online community on reddit called Wall Street Bets bought positions on companies that billions dollars hedge funds shorted such as Gamestop. By buying these positions they made these institutional investors to buy their short positions back but at an inflated price to recover losses.

Insight

  Many of the people in this community of Wall Street Bets are considered retail investors or individual investors because they are not professional investors. Whereas the billion dollar hedge funds who professionally trade on wall street are considered institutional investors. Applications that allow individual investors to trade stocks like Robinhood have recently been accused of limiting the trade of particularly volatile stocks, this is an interesting development in online platform ethics because it is altering general practices to benefit their company over the rights of the general users or individual investors.

  This practice, in turn, is allowing institutional investors and interests to profit off the platform's purposefully ignorant ideals. The problem here is that the increase in regulation is being supported by some in institutional investing. Until sites like Robinhood started limiting investing for volatile stocks, it still allowed individual investors to control the market in a limited capacity. Yet, regulation is being asked for by individuals because they feel limited from a free market through a platform that clearly advertises investing for anyone. This problem stems from historically one-sided markets that allow the rich to multiply their wealth and retail investors to miss out. With the revolutionary bounds in technology and new innovative platforms, the inclusion of personalized, individual investing became recognized. Unfortunately, this hasn't allowed markets to run smoothly for privatized interests and institutional investing. This underlying issue has been a fundamental problem in US markets and trading. For example, a popular movie Wolf of Wall Street it depicted how institutional firms and funds abused penny stocks in order to inflate particular stocks and profit off the individual investor. Moreover, the house market crash during the mid-2000's was another glaring example of how little regulation within investing can allow for very systematic problems. The real question to ask here is if this increased regulation will lead to more overwhelming problems within our public markets, or whether it will resolve deep rooted partial investing practices. From a technological standpoint, these services need to provide their users with what they are continually insisting they give their users the freedom to invest their money in a free market through their platform. It may be within their rights to limit purchase and sale of volatile stocks and bonds, but is the precedent that we want to nurture with other platforms and services. Clearly this has implications in other similar sectors as well, for example, misinformation is another major fault of platforms now. By pushing falsified information to particular audiences with algorithms, they are instigating wrong factual information and harmful opinions. This limitation of factual information similarly relates to this trading situation in that they are clearly revealing the need for increased regulation among online platforms. The more influential the service, the more influential they will be in other areas as well. Facebook and other platforms may have knowingly started hostilities during the election and following events with misinformation and private groupings. While Robinhood cost privatized hedge funds billions of dollars on short positions. Both show how influential the other is, they clearly have the power to change and alter opinion and current institutions as we know it. Therefore the fundamental need for a national sector dedicated to online regulation and ethical integrity is imminent.
